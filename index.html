Ancient-Long History in Short

Plovdiv, Bulgaria, boasts an ancient history spanning over 8,000 years. Founded by the Thracians in the 4th millennium BCE, it was known as Eumolpias and later Philippopolis under Philip II of Macedon. The city thrived under Roman rule, becoming a vital trade hub on the Via Diagonalis. In the Byzantine era, it was a significant Christian center.

In the Middle Ages, Plovdiv changed hands between the Byzantines, Bulgarians, and Crusaders. Ottoman Turks conquered it in 1364, leading to centuries of Ottoman rule. The city played a role in the Bulgarian National Revival, contributing to Bulgaria's fight for independence.

Today, Plovdiv's ancient heritage is evident in its well-preserved Roman amphitheater, ancient walls, and charming Old Town. It also holds the title of one of the world's oldest continuously inhabited cities, making it a testament to the enduring legacy of its rich history.

The Old Town

The Old Town in Plovdiv, Bulgaria, is a charming and historic district that reflects the city's rich heritage. Dating back over 6,000 years, it is one of Europe's oldest continuously inhabited areas. This well-preserved architectural gem boasts a captivating blend of Roman, Byzantine, Ottoman, and Bulgarian influences. Cobblestone streets wind through a maze of colorful houses, many adorned with intricate frescoes. The highlight is the Ancient Theatre of Philippopolis, a 2nd-century AD Roman amphitheater still used for cultural events.

The Old Town is a cultural hub with numerous art galleries, museums, and quaint cafes that line its picturesque streets. It offers visitors a glimpse into Plovdiv's history and a unique atmosphere where the past seamlessly meets the present. In 2019, it earned the prestigious title of European Capital of Culture, further enhancing its status as a must-visit destination for history and culture enthusiasts.

Enjoying the Hills

Plovdiv, Bulgaria, is characterized by its stunning hills, which contribute to the city's unique charm and landscape. The most prominent are the "Three Hills" - Nebet Tepe, Dzhendem Tepe, and Taksim Tepe. Nebet Tepe, also known as the Hill of the Guards, is the oldest and offers panoramic views of the city. Dzhendem Tepe is famous for its historical landmarks, including the ancient Stadium of Philippopolis. Taksim Tepe provides a picturesque park and a more modern perspective on Plovdiv.

These hills serve as natural focal points for both locals and tourists, offering not only historic and cultural significance but also recreational opportunities like hiking and leisurely walks. They are dotted with archaeological sites, monuments, and green spaces, making them integral to Plovdiv's identity and a testament to the city's rich history. Whether exploring the past or enjoying the scenic beauty, Plovdiv's hills are a captivating aspect of this Bulgarian city.

Dinner at Kapana

Dining in Plovdiv's Kapana District is a delightful culinary experience. Kapana, meaning "The Trap," is a vibrant and trendy area known for its diverse food scene. This artistic neighborhood is teeming with charming cafes, bars, and restaurants offering a wide range of international and Bulgarian cuisine.

Visitors can savor traditional Bulgarian dishes like kebapche (grilled minced meat), banitsa (flaky pastry filled with cheese or meat), and shopska salad, as well as international options, including Italian, Asian, and Mediterranean cuisine. The district's eateries often feature creative menus, cozy atmospheres, and outdoor seating, perfect for enjoying the picturesque surroundings.

Kapana is not only a gastronomic hub but also a cultural hotspot, with its vibrant street art and events. It's a must-visit destination for food enthusiasts looking to explore Plovdiv's diverse culinary offerings in a lively and artistic setting.
